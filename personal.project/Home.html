Prishtina’s pour-over coffee culture ☕️ 6 In Pristina, coffee isn’t just a drink—it’s a daily ritual. Traditionally, people here leaned heavily on strong espresso-style drinks (macchiatos are especially popular), influenced by Balkan and Italian café culture. But over the last decade, a quieter shift has been happening: specialty cafés have introduced pour-over coffee—a slower, more deliberate way of brewing that highlights flavor clarity and origin. In these newer cafés, you’ll see baristas using tools like the V60 or Chemex, carefully pouring hot water over freshly ground beans. It’s a contrast to the fast-paced espresso culture—more about craftsmanship, patience, and taste exploration. Younger coffee enthusiasts in Prishtina have embraced it as part of a broader global trend toward artisanal food and drink. Where pour-over coffee actually comes from 6 Pour-over coffee might feel modern, but its roots go back over a century. In 1908, a German woman named Melitta Bentz invented the first paper coffee filter. Frustrated with bitter coffee and grounds in her cup, she punched holes in a brass pot and lined it with blotting paper from her son’s notebook. That simple idea became the foundation for manual drip brewing—and eventually the company Melitta. From there, pour-over evolved into different forms: Ceramic and glass drippers Spiral designs like the V60 (developed in Japan decades later) Thick paper filters that create clean, tea-like coffee How it spread globally (and reached Prishtina) 7 Pour-over didn’t become globally trendy until much later. In the late 20th and early 21st centuries, the “third wave coffee movement” emphasized quality, origin, and brewing technique. Japanese brands like Hario refined pour-over tools, turning them into precision instruments. Specialty cafés in Europe and beyond adopted the method as a way to showcase single-origin beans. Eventually, that wave reached cities like Prishtina. As travel, social media, and global food culture expanded, local cafés began experimenting with the same techniques you’d find in Berlin, London, or New York. Why people in Prishtina are getting into it Pour-over appeals to a different mindset than traditional coffee: Flavor clarity: You can taste fruity, floral, or chocolate notes more distinctly Control: Baristas adjust grind size, temperature, and pouring technique Experience: It’s slower and more intentional—almost meditative That said, it hasn’t replaced espresso culture—it’s just added another layer. You’ll still see packed cafés serving macchiatos all day, but alongside them, a growing niche of people are exploring coffee like wine.
